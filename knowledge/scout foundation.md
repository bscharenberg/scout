## The Fictional Company: BTO Group

**Full name:** BTO Group
**Tagline:** *We'll get to the bottom of it.*
**What everyone assumes BTO stands for:** Something prestigious.
**What it actually stands for:** Boil The Ocean.

BTO Group is a mid-sized strategy and transformation consultancy founded in 2011. Headquartered in Seattle with offices in New York, Austin, and London. The firm employs ~380 consultants across four practice areas and competes squarely against the Big 4 advisory arms (Deloitte, PwC, KPMG, EY) and strategy boutiques like Kearney and West Monroe.

BTO's market position is deliberately paradoxical: a firm named after the consulting anti-pattern, staffed by people who've escaped larger firms, selling the promise that they won't do the thing their name implies. That self-awareness is real and it shows up in how BTO people talk - direct, occasionally self-deprecating, allergic to jargon they can't back up.

**Two buyer segments:**
- **Enterprise clients** ($1B+ revenue): C-suite relationships, multi-year transformations, ACV $800K-$3M+
- **Mid-market clients** ($150M-$1B revenue): VP/Director-level buyers, scoped engagements, ACV $150K-$600K

**Primary sales motion:** Relationship and referral-led. BTO doesn't win RFPs it didn't shape. Most new logos come through partner networks, alumni of client firms, and PE sponsor relationships.

**Revenue model:** Fixed-fee project engagements, retainer-based advisory, and outcome-linked structures for select PE-backed clients.

**Competitive differentiator:** The "Ground Truth" methodology - every engagement starts with unfiltered diagnosis before recommendations. BTO's pitch is that most consultancies arrive with an answer and look for evidence. BTO finds the answer in the evidence. Whether that's always true is another matter, but the reps believe it, and that matters.

**Industries:** Financial services, healthcare systems, industrial manufacturing, PE portfolio companies, business services.

## BTO Brand Identity & Voice

*This is the Identity Layer - it underlies every Scout interaction and is also directly queryable by reps who are ramping.*

### Who BTO Is

BTO is the firm for buyers who've been burned before. They've had the Big 4 team - large, junior-heavy, expensive, and gone the moment the final presentation was delivered. They've had the boutique that was brilliant on diagnosis and useless on implementation. BTO's identity is built on a simple belief: **the gap between insight and action is where consulting value goes to die**, and closing that gap is the only thing worth charging for.

BTO people are:
- **Direct** - they say what they think, including when the answer isn't what the client wants to hear
- **Earned-confidence, not performative confidence** - they don't project certainty they don't have
- **Self-aware about the industry** - they know the jokes about consultants and they've earned the right to make them
- **Delivery-oriented** - they're more interested in what happens after the presentation than during it

### Voice & Tone

**In client conversations:**
- Plain language over consulting vocabulary. Say "decision-making" not "decisioning." Say "we think" not "our hypothesis is."
- Short sentences when the point is important. If you're using a long sentence, it should be because the idea genuinely requires it.
- Confident but not closed. BTO reps share a point of view and invite pushback - they don't defend a position they can't back up.
- Humor is allowed. Light self-deprecation about the consulting industry is on-brand. Sarcasm at the client's expense is never on-brand.

**In written materials (proposals, SOWs, follow-ups):**
- Lead with the client's situation, not BTO's credentials
- Structure matters more than length - a well-organized two-pager beats a comprehensive deck
- Avoid: "leverage," "synergies," "robust," "holistic," "best-in-class," "world-class," "cutting-edge," "seamlessly"
- Use instead: specific verbs, concrete nouns, actual numbers where available

**Phrases BTO people actually say:**
- "Here's what we're seeing, and here's what we'd do about it."
- "We'd rather be right than comprehensive."
- "The answer is in the room - we help you find it and act on it."
- "We're not here to tell you things you already know. We're here to help you do something about them."

**Phrases BTO people do not say:**
- "Circle back" (ironic, given the industry)
- "Boiling the ocean" (obviously)
- "Ping me" (unless texting a close client)
- "Move the needle" unironically
- "At the end of the day" more than once per conversation

### The BTO Name - How to Handle It

Reps get asked about the name. The answer is always some version of: *"We named the firm after the thing we're not supposed to do. It keeps us honest."* This lands well because it's disarming. Don't oversell it. One line, then move on.

```

Triggered when a rep is ramping and needs to understand how BTO talks, positions itself, or shows up in conversations and written materials.

- Self-aware. The firm knows it's a consultancy. It doesn't pretend to be something else.

## Practice Area Summaries

### Practice Area 1: Organizational Strategy

**What it is:** Helps leadership teams make high-stakes directional decisions - where to play, how to win, how to align the organization behind a clear agenda.

**Typical triggers:**
- New CEO or leadership transition
- Stalled growth or margin pressure with no clear diagnosis
- Board or PE sponsor pushing for strategic clarity
- Portfolio complexity after M&A activity

**Flagship offering:** The Ground Truth Sprint - 6 weeks, fixed fee. Delivers a current-state assessment, a prioritized strategic agenda, and an executive-aligned point of view on the three to five decisions that matter most. BTO tells you what they think, not just what the data says.

**Why BTO over Big 4:** Big 4 strategy arms produce comprehensive frameworks. BTO produces a point of view. The deliverable is a decision, not a document.

**Common objection:** "We already have an internal strategy team."
**Response frame:** BTO isn't replacing internal capability - they're providing external pressure-testing and the ability to surface things that are politically difficult to say internally. The internal team runs the business; BTO helps them see it clearly.

---

### Practice Area 2: Operating Model Design

**What it is:** Helps organizations redesign how they operate - structures, accountabilities, decision rights, ways of working - to execute strategy more effectively.

**Typical triggers:**
- Post-merger integration requiring structural alignment
- Rapid scale creating organizational strain
- Cost reduction mandate paired with performance expectations
- Digital transformation creating new capabilities that don't fit old org designs

**Flagship offering:** Operating Model Blueprint - 10-14 weeks. Current-state diagnosis, design options, leadership alignment workshop, implementation roadmap with decision rights framework.

**Why BTO over Big 4:** BTO's operating model work is tightly coupled to strategy - they don't redesign org charts in isolation. The design is always anchored to what the business is actually trying to accomplish.

**Common objection:** "We've done org redesigns before and they didn't stick."
**Response frame:** Acknowledge it directly - most don't stick. The reason is almost always the same: design happened without enough attention to decision rights and without enough involvement from the people who have to live in the new model. BTO builds implementation specificity into the Blueprint from week one, not as a follow-on.

---

### Practice Area 3: Digital & Data Transformation

**What it is:** Helps organizations define and execute technology and data strategies - not platform implementation, but the strategy, governance, and operating model required to make digital transformation actually work.

**Typical triggers:**
- Failed or stalled digital programs with no clear owner
- Data fragmentation across business units blocking insight
- New CTO or CDO building a credible transformation roadmap
- Board-level pressure to show a coherent digital agenda

**Flagship offering:** Digital Readiness Assessment + Roadmap - 8 weeks. Prioritized transformation roadmap, capability gap analysis, governance model, sequenced investment recommendations.

**Why BTO over Big 4:** BTO doesn't implement technology, so there's no platform bias. Recommendations are based on what the business needs, not what generates downstream implementation revenue for the advisor.

**Common objection:** "We need someone who can do the implementation too."
**Response frame:** That's exactly the conflict of interest worth surfacing. An advisor with implementation revenue at stake will recommend the solution that creates the most work for them. BTO helps the client make the right decision - then the client chooses the best implementer without BTO in the room. Independence is the value, not a limitation.

---

### Practice Area 4: M&A Integration

**What it is:** Helps acquirers plan and execute the integration of acquired companies - particularly the operating model, culture, and capability integration that determines whether deals deliver their thesis.

**Typical triggers:**
- Completed or imminent acquisition
- PE sponsor managing a buy-and-build portfolio thesis
- History of acquisitions that underdelivered on synergy targets
- Integration team overwhelmed or structurally under-resourced

**Flagship offering:** Integration Accelerator - begins pre-close, runs through 100-day and 12-month milestones. Covers IMO setup, synergy tracking, operating model decisions, and culture alignment framework.

**Why BTO over Big 4:** Speed and decisiveness. Big 4 integration teams are large and process-heavy. BTO brings a smaller senior team that makes decisions faster - critical in the first 90 days when most integration value is won or lost.

**Common objection:** "The deal hasn't closed yet - it's too early."
**Response frame:** That's actually the most dangerous time to wait. The decisions made in the 60 days before close determine the first 100 days after it. BTO can engage pre-close with a light-touch planning sprint that doesn't disrupt the transaction and dramatically reduces Day 1 risk.

## Persona Cards

### Persona 1: The Enterprise CFO (Economic Buyer)

**Archetype names:** David Chen, Sarah Okafor, Marcus Webb
**Title:** CFO, Chief Financial Officer
**Company:** $1B-$5B revenue, financial services / industrial / healthcare

**What they care about:**
- ROI clarity before committing budget - they want to know what they're buying
- Risk mitigation - they've been burned by engagements that overpromised
- Speed to value - fiscal year pressures make 18-month timelines unacceptable
- The actual team, not the pitch team

**How they buy:**
- Rarely initiates - gets pulled in to approve scope and budget
- Makes decisions in one or two conversations, not ten
- High procurement involvement - expect RFP or competitive process at this level
- Will ask the hard question once; if the answer is weak they won't ask again

**What makes them say yes:**
- Specific outcomes by specific dates
- Reference points from companies at similar scale and complexity
- Fixed-fee structure that limits financial exposure
- The sense that BTO won't disappear after the presentation

**What makes them say no:**
- Vague outcome framing ("we'll help you think through your strategy")
- Suspicion that the senior partner won't be in the room after week two
- Feeling managed rather than advised

---

### Persona 2: The Mid-Market VP of Strategy or COO (Champion + Decision-Maker)

**Archetype names:** Jenny Park, Marcus Reid, Priya Nair
**Title:** VP Strategy, COO, SVP Operations
**Company:** $200M-$800M revenue, healthcare services / business services / specialty manufacturing

**What they care about:**
- Getting credit for bringing in a firm that actually helped
- Protecting their internal credibility - if the engagement fails, it reflects on them
- Practical outputs they can act on, not frameworks to file
- Being able to explain the investment to their CEO in one sentence

**How they buy:**
- Often the initiator and the decision-maker
- Wants to meet the actual team before committing - not the sales team
- Limited budget authority - will often need to build the internal case
- Personal relationship matters more than brand name at this level

**What makes them say yes:**
- They trust the person in front of them
- The scope feels right-sized - not a $2M engagement when a $350K one would do
- Strong references from companies at a similar growth stage
- They can picture themselves presenting the work product to the board

**What makes them say no:**
- Feels like a templated Big 4 proposal with a smaller logo
- Unclear what's actually being delivered for the fee
- Not convinced the senior person they met will do the work

---

### Persona 3: The Internal Skeptic / Blocker

**Role:** Head of Internal Strategy, incumbent vendor loyalist, procurement lead
**Stakes:** Can slow or kill a deal without being the economic buyer

**Common blocking moves:**
- "We should run a formal RFP"
- "We've worked with [Big 4 firm] for years - why would we change?"
- "Our internal team can handle this"
- "The timing doesn't work right now"

**How to handle:**
- Don't fight the blocker directly - find the champion and help them navigate internally
- For RFP pressure: agree to participate but accelerate the economic buyer relationship in parallel
- For Big 4 loyalty: probe the dissatisfaction (there's almost always some) without being explicitly competitive
- For internal team objection: reframe as complementary - BTO brings external perspective and air cover, not headcount replacement
- For timing: clarify whether it's a budget issue or an alignment issue, then address the real one

## Objection Bank

| Objection | Response Frame |
|---|---|
| "You're too expensive compared to the Big 4." | The Big 4 price looks lower at proposal stage - it rarely is at invoice stage. The difference is staffing: their pricing assumes a pyramid of junior analysts managed by a senior partner who shows up for milestone reviews. Our pricing reflects the team that actually does the work. |
| "We've used consultants before and the work sat on a shelf." | That's the most common outcome in this industry, and it happens for a predictable reason: the work is done *for* the client, not *with* them. Our Ground Truth methodology is explicitly built around co-development. We don't present findings - we build the answer together with the people who have to implement it. |
| "We need deeper industry expertise." | What specifically are you trying to solve? Industry expertise matters most for benchmarking and market knowledge - we can cover that. For operating model and strategy work, the limiting factor is almost never industry knowledge. It's the ability to ask the right questions and build alignment across a leadership team that disagrees about the answer. |
| "The timing isn't right - maybe next quarter." | Timing objections usually signal one of two things: budget isn't secured, or internal alignment isn't there yet. Which one is it for you? I'd rather help you solve the real blocker than schedule a call for Q2. |
| "We want more case studies from our specific industry." | We can share relevant reference work. I'd also push back gently: the most useful references are companies that faced the same *type* of problem, not necessarily the same industry. The structural similarities between a $400M healthcare services company and a $400M specialty manufacturer are often more relevant than the differences. |
| "We're going to run an RFP." | We'll participate. And I'd ask one question: is this a genuine competitive process, or is procurement requiring a process around a decision that's already directionally made? If it's the former, we want to compete. If it's the latter, let's talk about how to make this efficient for everyone. |
| "Your firm is too small for an engagement of this size." | Size matters for staffing risk - we understand that concern. The question is whether you want a large team or the right team. Our model is intentionally lean and senior-heavy. If you want 40 consultants on-site, we're not the right fit. If you want the six people who'll actually solve the problem, let's talk about who we'd put on this. |

## BTO Deal Shaping Guide

*Reference material for Scout's Deal Shaping mode.*

### Proposal Structure (Standard)

1. **Situation** - mirror the client's problem back to them in their language. If they feel understood, they'll read the rest.
2. **What we've heard** - key themes from discovery. Demonstrates listening, not just pitching.
3. **Our point of view** - what we believe is true about this situation, including anything uncomfortable. This is the BTO differentiator.
4. **Proposed approach** - phases, workstreams, milestones. Keep it tight. Three phases max for a first engagement.
5. **Team** - name the people, show their relevant experience, be specific. No "supported by our bench of experts."
6. **Investment** - fixed fee with clear payment milestones. Optional: a phased entry point (e.g., diagnostic sprint before full engagement).
7. **Why now** - one paragraph on what's at risk by waiting. Not fear-mongering - honest framing.

### SOW Key Elements

- **Scope definition:** What is explicitly included. What is explicitly excluded (just as important).
- **Deliverables:** Specific, named outputs with formats (e.g., "Operating Model Blueprint - a 25-35 page structured document and accompanying decision rights matrix").
- **Milestones and payment schedule:** Tie payments to deliverable completion, not calendar dates.
- **Access requirements:** What BTO needs from the client - stakeholder time, data access, workspace. Under-specified access is the #1 source of engagement delays.
- **Change order process:** How scope changes are identified, priced, and approved. Establish this in the SOW, not after the first change request.
- **IP and confidentiality:** BTO retains methodology IP; client owns all deliverables specific to their engagement. This is the standard position.

### Procurement & Legal Landmines by Buyer Type

**Enterprise (Big Co, in-house legal):**
- Expect their paper (MSA + SOW template). Push to use BTO's SOW for scope clarity; accept their MSA if needed.
- Watch for: unlimited liability clauses, broad IP assignment language, non-solicitation clauses that are too wide, payment terms of Net 90+.
- Standard BTO position: liability capped at fees paid in prior 6 months; IP assignment limited to client-specific deliverables; payment Net 30.

**Mid-market (lean legal, often outside counsel):**
- More flexibility on paper - often willing to use BTO's standard agreement.
- Watch for: personal guarantee requests from PE-backed companies, change-of-control clauses that could affect continuity, vague scope that creates scope creep risk.

**PE-backed portfolio companies:**
- Sponsor may require vendor approval at the fund level - identify this early.
- IP language from PE sponsors is often aggressive (they want to own everything). Hold the line on methodology IP.
- Payment risk is real in distressed portfolio situations - consider milestone-heavy payment schedule.

### Pricing Principles

- **Fixed fee is the default.** Time-and-materials opens BTO to scope creep accusations and creates misaligned incentives.
- **Phase 1 entry points work.** A $75K-$120K diagnostic sprint lowers the barrier to a first engagement and almost always leads to Phase 2.
- **Don't discount the fee - adjust the scope.** If the buyer needs a lower number, reduce scope rather than applying a percentage discount. Discounting trains clients to expect it and devalues the work.
- **Outcome-linked components** (5-15% of total fee tied to defined outcomes) can work for the right client relationship but require extremely precise outcome definition in the SOW. Don't propose this casually.
